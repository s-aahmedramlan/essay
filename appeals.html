Appeal Letters

Understanding when and how to appeal an admission decision

⚠️ Important: Appeals Are Rarely Successful

It's important to understand that admission appeals are rarely granted. Most colleges have strict policies, and successful appeals typically require significant new information or evidence of procedural errors. However, in certain circumstances, a well-crafted appeal letter may be appropriate.

What is an Appeal Letter?

An appeal letter is a formal request to have an admission decision reconsidered. This is different from a waitlist letter (LOCI). Appeals are typically only appropriate when there are specific grounds for reconsideration, such as new information that wasn't available during the initial review, procedural errors, or extenuating circumstances that significantly impacted your application.

When an Appeal May Be Appropriate

Appeals are most likely to be considered in these specific circumstances:

1. Significant New Information

You have substantial new achievements, awards, or information that wasn't available when you submitted your application and that significantly strengthens your candidacy.

2. Procedural Errors

There was a clear error in how your application was processed or reviewed (e.g., missing materials that were submitted, incorrect information in your file).

3. Extenuating Circumstances

Serious personal, family, or medical circumstances that significantly impacted your academic performance or application, and that you couldn't adequately explain in your original application.

4. School-Specific Appeal Policies

Some schools have formal appeal processes. If a school explicitly allows appeals and provides guidelines, following those guidelines precisely is essential.

When NOT to Appeal

  • ✗ Simply disagreeing with the decision
  • ✗ Minor updates or achievements that don't significantly change your profile
  • ✗ The school doesn't accept appeals (many top schools don't)
  • ✗ Emotional appeals without substantive grounds

Key Components of an Effective Appeal Letter

1. Professional, Respectful Tone

Maintain a formal, respectful, and professional tone throughout. Avoid emotional language, complaints, or accusations. The letter should demonstrate maturity and understanding of the admissions process.

2. Clear Statement of Purpose

Immediately state that you're requesting an appeal and briefly explain the grounds (new information, procedural error, etc.). Be direct and clear about why you believe reconsideration is warranted.

3. Substantive New Information

If appealing based on new information, provide:

  • • Specific, significant achievements or updates
  • • Documentation or evidence supporting your claims
  • • Explanation of why this information is substantial and relevant

4. Documentation

Include supporting documentation such as award certificates, updated transcripts, letters of recommendation, or other relevant materials that substantiate your appeal.

5. Compliance with School Policies

Follow the school's specific appeal process exactly. This may include deadlines, required forms, submission methods, and specific information to include. Failure to follow procedures will result in automatic denial.

Important Considerations

  • • Check if appeals are accepted: Many top universities do not accept appeals. Verify the school's policy before proceeding.
  • • Respect deadlines: Appeal deadlines are typically very strict. Submit well before the deadline.
  • • Be realistic: Understand that even well-crafted appeals are rarely successful. Have a backup plan.
  • • One appeal only: Most schools will only consider one appeal per application cycle.
